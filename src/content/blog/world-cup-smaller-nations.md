Football has always had its elite. Brazil, Germany, France, Spain, Argentina — the names that are supposed to matter come a major tournament. But over the last few years, something has shifted. Smaller nations are no longer turning up just to make the numbers. They are winning games they have no business winning, and in doing so, they are forcing a rethink of what international football actually looks like.

## Qatar 2022: Morocco and the Moment Everything Changed

If there was one team at the 2022 World Cup that captured the world's imagination, it was Morocco. They came into the tournament ranked 22nd in the world — respectable, but not the kind of ranking that makes people pencil you in for the semi-finals.

Then they went and reached them anyway.

Morocco beat Spain on penalties in the Round of 16. They knocked out Portugal — Cristiano Ronaldo and all — in the quarter-finals. They became the first African nation, and the first Arab nation, to ever reach a World Cup semi-final. The scenes in Casablanca, Rabat, and across the Arab world after each victory were extraordinary. This was not a fluke result against a tired opponent. This was a team with a defensive structure, a game plan, and the collective belief to execute it across six matches.

But Morocco were not the only story. Japan beat Germany and Spain in the group stage — *Germany and Spain* — and only went out to Croatia on penalties in the last 16. Saudi Arabia beat Argentina in what many consider the biggest upset in World Cup history. Australia reached the quarter-finals, beating Denmark along the way. Cameroon beat Brazil, though neither side had much riding on it by that point.

The pattern was impossible to ignore. The tournament hierarchy was not working the way it was supposed to.

## Euro 2020: The Warning Signs Were Already There

To understand why none of this should have come as a surprise, you only need to look back at Euro 2020 — played in 2021, but we will not get into that.

The tournament is remembered for some extraordinary football, Italy's deserved victory, and England's heartbreaking penalty shootout defeat in the final. But the subplot that did not get enough attention was just how badly some of the supposed giants performed.

France arrived at Euro 2020 as reigning World Cup champions. They had a squad most international managers would trade their house for — Mbappé, Benzema, Pogba, Griezmann. They went out in the Round of 16 to Switzerland. On penalties. Switzerland, a nation of 8 million people, eliminated the world champions.

Germany, one of the most decorated sides in football history, lost to England in the Round of 16 and crashed out. Portugal, with Ronaldo in arguably the best form of his career at that point, were beaten by Belgium in the same round.

Meanwhile, Denmark — who had been rocked by Christian Eriksen's cardiac arrest on the pitch in their opening game — somehow rallied, won their group, and reached the semi-finals. Czech Republic also made the last eight. These are not tiny nations, but they are not the names traditionally associated with tournament glory either.

## Why Is This Happening?

A few things are converging to make this the new normal.

**Data and coaching have spread.** Tactical knowledge that once lived only in the top clubs and national setups is now accessible everywhere. A well-coached defensive block from a smaller nation can neutralise a side with far superior individual talent.

**Club football has raised the floor.** Players from nations like Morocco, Japan, and Senegal are playing week-in, week-out in the Premier League, La Liga, and the Bundesliga. They arrive at major tournaments battle-hardened and technically refined in a way that previous generations simply were not.

**The big nations have their own problems.** Bloated squads, political infighting, and the pressure of expectation can be paralysing. France at Euro 2020 is a perfect example — so much talent, so little cohesion.

**Smaller nations play without fear.** When you are not expected to win, you can be free. Morocco in Qatar, Denmark at the Euros — these teams played with an intensity and togetherness that money alone cannot buy.

## What This Means Going Forward

The old way of thinking about international tournaments — where a handful of nations share the trophy between them and everyone else is a supporting act — is increasingly outdated. The competition is genuinely open in a way it has never been before.

That is not a threat to the beauty of the game. It is an argument for it.

The best tournaments are the ones where you cannot predict the outcome. Where a nation of 36 million people beats the world champions. Where a goalkeeper from a country that most fans could not find on a map becomes a hero to an entire continent.

Football is at its best when it surprises you. And right now, it is doing that more than ever.
